Throwing Drills

Mechanics


  1. *Wrist Snaps
    • Purpose

      • Increase power, accuracy, and develop wrist strength and flexibility.


    • Equipment
      • Ball
      • Glove


    • Organization

      • Groups of two work best. This eliment can be added to players' throwing warmup routine.


    • Execution

      • Resting the glove hand under the throwing hand's elbow, the player flicks the ball toward their partner, whom is ready to catch the ball with two hands. The flicking player should not extend their forearm or rotate at the shoulder. The forearm from the elbow up should remain still. The flicking motion occurs at the wrist, while using proper grip on the ball. Proper wrist snaps will result in the ball moving in a forward, linear motion toward their partner's glove.


    • Variations
      1. Use a striped ball to see the balls rotation.
      2. Use a weighted ball to increase wrist strength.
      3. Vary the type of ball used in the wrist snap if hand size or strength is an issue.
      4. Can be done without partners, alone. Have the player lay on their back, snapping the ball into the air above them, allowing the ball to return with the force of gravity. Repeat this series for repetition.

    • Coaching Points

      • Emphasize that this is one of the last parts of a successful throw. The ball should roll off of the fingers, where the pads of the finger tip release, or snap, the seams of the ball. Proper roation will appear to be bottom-to-top, disecting the diameter of the ball. Both the thrower and the catcher should observe the spin to apply in sections beyond throwing.

  3. *Wrist Snaps w/ Striped Ball
    • Purpose

      • Increase power, accuracy, develop wrist strength and flexibility, and observe a thrown ball's rotation.


    • Equipment
      • Ball with electrical tape or marker line drawn across the diameter
      • Glove


    • Organization

      • Groups of two work best. This eliment can be added to players' throwing warmup routine.


    • Execution

      • Resting the glove hand under the throwing hand's elbow, the player flicks the ball toward their partner, whom is ready to catch the ball with two hands. The flicking player should not extend their forearm or rotate at the shoulder. The forearm from the elbow up should remain still. The flicking motion occurs at the wrist, while using proper grip on the ball. Proper wrist snaps will result in the ball moving in a forward, linear motion toward their partner's glove. Players should notice the movement, or lack of movement, of the black line on the ball. If done properly, the line will appear to be straight through the middle of the ball, not moving side-to-side.


    • Variations
      1. Use a weighted ball to increase wrist strength.
      2. Vary the type of ball used in the wrist snap if hand size or strength is an issue.
      3. Can be done without partners, alone. Have the player lay on their back, snapping the ball into the air above them, allowing the ball to return with the force of gravity. Repeat this series for repetition.

    • Coaching Points

      • Emphasize that this is one of the last parts of a successful throw. The ball should roll off of the fingers, where the pads of the finger tip release, or snap, the seams of the ball. Proper roation will appear to be bottom-to-top, disecting the diameter of the ball. Both the thrower and the catcher should observe the spin to apply in sections beyond throwing.
